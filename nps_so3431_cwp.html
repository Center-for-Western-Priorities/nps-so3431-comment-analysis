NPS Secretary Order 3431  ·  Public Comment Analysis

National park visitors flooded the NPS snitch form… but not to snitch on parks

The Trump administration posted QR codes at national parks asking visitors to report signs that “disparage” American history. An analysis of 35,700 submitted comments shows that less than 1% actually used those forms as intended.

Center for Western Priorities  ·  Data: NPS SO-3431 public comments released via FOIA  ·  June 2025–January 2026  ·  475 park units

In March 2025, President Trump signed an executive order directing federal agencies to review how American history is presented at National Park Service sites, with a mandate to eliminate content that “inappropriately disparages Americans past or living.” Interior Secretary Doug Burgum followed with Secretary Order 3431, which directed NPS staff to post QR code signs at every park unit asking visitors to report interpretive content that failed to “emphasize the beauty, grandeur, and abundance of landscapes and other natural features.”

The move drew immediate criticism from historians, conservation groups, and the general public who identified it as a mechanism to erase or whitewash the difficult parts of American history, including exhibits on slavery, Indigenous displacement, Japanese American incarceration, and climate change. Reports of signs and exhibits being flagged or removed soon followed at dozens of parks and historic sites.

In May 2026, the Department of the Interior released 35,700 comments submitted through the QR code system in response to a FOIA request by KOAA News 5. The Center for Western Priorities analyzed the full dataset to understand how the public actually used the form and found that almost no one used it as the Trump administration intended. Comments flooded in and included mostly general support for protecting national parks, arguments for the preservation of history, general opposition to the secretarial order and the form itself, reviews of visits to national parks, opposition to Trump and Burgum, and a wide array of jokes and trolling. But a negligible amount of comments, about 0.1 percent, actually reported signage or supported the removal of signage. The American people certainly took advantage of this public input process and made their voices heard, and they are vehemently opposed to attempts to erase history and censor national parks.

Fewer than 1 in 800 comments flagged a sign for removal — the form’s stated purpose
That’s 47 comments out of 35,700 submitted across 475 national park units between June 2025 and January 2026 — verified through a complete manual review.

Of the 35,700 total submissions, 13,308 were coordinated form letters — verbatim or near-verbatim campaigns distributed by advocacy organizations and submitted across hundreds of parks. Every single identified campaign was in opposition to the sign-removal order. Even setting those aside and looking only at the 22,392 original, individual comments, just 47 (≈0.2%) were confirmed — through a complete manual review — as flagging a specific sign or supporting sign removal. The remaining 99.8% defended historical accuracy, expressed support for the NPS, or pushed back against the order.

Of those 47, only 13 were using the form in the way the Trump administration likely intended — calling for removal of content they considered too biased toward Indigenous peoples, people of color, climate science, or LGBTQ history. 17 used the form to demand more inclusive history and better representation in signage, such as noting that a historical marker used a racial slur. Several seemed to be satirical, and some flagged signs for being poorly written or having factual inaccuracies. The breakdown is shown below.

Methodology

Data source

The 35,700-row dataset was released May 22, 2026 by the Department of the Interior in response to a FOIA request filed by KOAA News 5. The full dataset is available via the National Park Service FOIA request site. CWP conducted its own analysis with the assistance of Claude AI and a manual verification process.

On coordinated template comments

13,308 submissions were identified as coordinated campaigns — verbatim or near-verbatim texts submitted across hundreds of parks. All identified campaigns were in opposition to the sign-removal order. These are displayed separately in the chart above and excluded from the by-state breakdown. The 0.1% figure for flagged comments is calculated against the full 35,700-comment total.

Classification approach

Each original comment was classified using a rule-based pattern-matching script written in Python, developed with the assistance of Claude AI and verified by CWP staff. The classifier first identifies coordinated template campaigns, then assigns the remaining ~22,400 original comments to one of seven categories based on keyword and phrase matching. The full annotated script is available on CWP’s GitHub.

Category definitions

  • Defend historical accuracy — History is the primary subject: a substantive argument for why accurate, complete history matters and should be preserved in national parks.
  • General opposition to the order — Opposes the QR sign, the order, or the administration’s direction without making history the primary focus. The largest single category and a known catch-all; likely overlaps with defend-history and pro-parks sentiment.
  • General pro-parks support — Broad appreciation for the NPS as an institution; advocacy for funding, staffing, or protection of public lands.
  • Park visit experience — Feedback about a specific visit — facilities, trails, rangers, wildlife — without broader policy advocacy.
  • Trump / Burgum criticism — Primarily personal criticism of named officials, or criticism of policies not related to parks, public lands, or history.
  • Off-topic / jokes / spam — Nonsensical, sarcastic, trolling, or entirely unrelated entries.
  • Flagged signage or supported removal — Comments that used the form as directed: identifying a specific sign or exhibit for review, or expressing support for the order’s sign-removal direction. Note that some comments in this category called for more inclusive signage rather than less — they used the form to report signs, though not in the way the administration likely intended.

Accuracy and verification

There is ambiguity in many of the comments, which address multiple categories, and the final classification is subjective. A random sample of 120 comments (20 per category) was reviewed by CWP staff, with an overall agreement rate of approximately 89% in the most recent verification round (120 comments, 20 per category), after iterative improvements across six rounds of human review. Per-category accuracy ranged from 75% (general opposition to the order, a known catch-all) to 100% (Trump / Burgum criticism and defend historical accuracy). The low rate on general opposition reflects its broad catch-all design and does not affect the headline finding. The “flagged signage or supported removal” category was verified through a complete manual review by CWP staff of all 79 AI-categorized entries. “General opposition to the order” is a catch-all category and may overlap with “defend historical accuracy” and “general pro-parks support.”

State mapping

Park names were matched to states using a lookup table covering approximately 75% of submissions. The remainder did not have a park name associated with them or the park name could not be matched to a state.
